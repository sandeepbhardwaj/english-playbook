## Overview

English grammar becomes easier when you can tell whether a word is acting as a noun, verb, adjective, adverb, pronoun, preposition, conjunction, or determiner. Instead of memorizing labels in isolation, connect each label to its job in a real sentence. Remember that the same word can belong to different classes in different contexts, so always ask what job the word is doing here, not what it usually does.

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize the major word classes in authentic sentences.
- Separate form from function when a word changes category by use.
- Explain why the same word can behave differently across contexts.

## Key Rules

- A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea, while a verb shows action or state.
- Adjectives describe nouns; adverbs usually modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs.
- Determiners such as the, a, this, and many signal which noun you mean.
- Prepositions show relationships between nouns and other words (in, on, at, before, after).
- Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition (he, she, it, they, who, which).
- Conjunctions connect words or clauses (and, but, because, although, while).
- A word's form and its function are not always the same: **running** can be a verb form in *She is running* but a noun in *Running is good exercise*.
- Content words carry the main meaning (nouns, main verbs, adjectives, many adverbs); function words help organize grammar (determiners, pronouns, prepositions, conjunctions, auxiliaries).

## Quick Tests

- If a word can take **the, a, this, my, some** before it, it is often functioning as a noun: *the book, my idea*.
- If a word can change for tense or match the subject, it is probably a verb: *walk / walks / walked*.
- If a word answers **What kind? Which one? How many?**, it may be an adjective or determiner.
- If a word answers **How? When? Where? To what degree?**, it is often an adverb.
- If a word links one noun phrase to another element, it is often a preposition: *on the table, after lunch*.
- If a word joins two words, phrases, or clauses, it is a conjunction: *and, but, because, although*.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "The curious student quickly opened the grammar book."
*The (determiner), curious (adjective), student (noun), quickly (adverb), opened (verb), grammar book (noun phrase)*

**Example 2:** "She carefully explained the importance of practice."
*She (pronoun), carefully (adverb), explained (verb), the (determiner), importance (noun), of (preposition), practice (noun)*

**Example 3:** "Although it was raining, we went hiking near the mountain."
*Although (conjunction), it (pronoun), was raining (verb phrase), we (pronoun), near (preposition), the (determiner), mountain (noun)*

**Example 4:** "My friend Sam plays guitar and writes songs."
*My (determiner), friend (noun), Sam (proper noun), plays and writes (verbs), and (conjunction), songs (noun)*

**Example 5:** "The presentation was clear, but the examples were confusing."
*presentation (noun), was (linking verb), clear (adjective), but (conjunction), examples (noun), were (linking verb), confusing (adjective)*

**Example 6:** "The letter arrived yesterday, and I read it immediately."
*letter (noun), arrived (verb), yesterday (adverb), I (pronoun), it (pronoun), immediately (adverb)*

**Example 7:** "These helpful tips will improve your vocabulary quickly."
*These (determiner), helpful (adjective), tips (noun), will improve (verb), your (determiner), quickly (adverb)*

**Example 8:** "Between you and me, that new policy seems rather unfair."
*Between (preposition), you and me (pronouns), that (determiner), new (adjective), policy (noun), seems (linking verb), rather (adverb), unfair (adjective)*

**Example 9:** "Running daily strengthens both the mind and the body."
*Running (noun/gerund), daily (adverb), strengthens (verb), both (determiner), mind (noun), body (noun)*

**Example 10:** "The students who studied hard performed exceptionally well."
*students (noun), who (relative pronoun), studied (verb), hard (adverb), performed (verb), exceptionally (adverb), well (adverb)*

**Example 11:** "We **booked** a table after reading the **book**."
*booked (verb), a (determiner), table (noun), after (preposition), reading (verb form), the (determiner), book (noun)*

**Example 12:** "Before lunch, we walked around the park, but after lunch, we rested."
*Before / after (prepositions), walked / rested (verbs), but (conjunction), park / lunch (nouns)*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "She spoke friendly to the new student."
- ✅ RIGHT: "She spoke in a friendly way to the new student." / "She spoke kindly to the new student."
- Note: **Friendly** is usually an adjective, not a standard adverb.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "He ran quick to the store."
- ✅ RIGHT: "He ran quickly to the store."
- Note: Adverbs modify verbs; use quickly, not quick.

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "The news is exciting. She is exciting."
- ✅ RIGHT: "The news is exciting. She is excited."
- Note: Use the -ed adjective for the person's feeling, -ing for the thing's quality.

**Mistake 4:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Book me a table at restaurant."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Book me a table at the restaurant."
- Note: The definite article the is often needed before nouns.

**Mistake 5:**
- ❌ WRONG: "After we arrived, because the room was full."
- ✅ RIGHT: "After we arrived, we waited outside because the room was full."
- Note: **After** and **because** are doing different jobs. After introduces a time clause; because introduces a reason clause. Together they still need a complete main clause.

## Quick Reference

✓ NOUN: person, place, thing, idea (book, teacher, happiness)
✓ VERB: action or state (run, is, seem)
✓ ADJECTIVE: describes noun (beautiful, tall, boring)
✓ ADVERB: modifies verb/adjective/other adverb (quickly, very, finally)
✓ PRONOUN: replaces noun (he, she, they, who)
✓ DETERMINER: signals which noun (the, a, this, some)
✓ PREPOSITION: shows relationship (in, on, at, between, before)
✓ CONJUNCTION: connects words/clauses (and, but, because, although)

### A useful reminder

- **Before, after, since, and like** can belong to different classes depending on the sentence.
- Ask what the word is linking, modifying, or replacing before you label it.

## Practice Plan

1. Exercise 1 - Labeling: Read a paragraph and mark each word with its part of speech (noun, verb, adj, adv, etc.)
2. Exercise 2 - Identification: Identify the target word class in each sentence: 'Find the adverb that modifies ran.'
3. Exercise 3 - Word Transformation: Transform words to different classes (run→runner→running; quick→quickly)
4. Exercise 4 - Pairing: Create 10 pairs showing the same word used as two different parts of speech
5. Exercise 5 - Writing: Write five sentences about a daily activity, then label the word classes in each
6. Exercise 6 - Function Check: Choose five words such as **before, after, light, book, fast** and use each in two different ways

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: A Day at the Farmer's Market"

Watch how different parts of speech work together in a real-world scene.

"Every Saturday morning, Maya visits the busy farmer's market near downtown. She walks quickly between the colorful stalls, looking for fresh vegetables and ripe fruit."

"The vendors are quite friendly. They eagerly show their products and happily answer questions. 'These tomatoes are delicious and very fresh,' says one vendor proudly."

"Maya carefully selects several items: beautiful peppers, organic spinach, and fragrant herbs. She stands patiently at the payment table, where multiple customers wait together."

"Because it is a beautiful day, many people are shopping happily. Some chat while they walk; others take photos of the magnificent displays."

**Analysis:**

- Market (noun), Saturday (noun), Maya (noun) are the main subjects and objects.
- Visits, walks, show, answer, selects, stands are action verbs showing what people do.
- Busy, colorful, fresh, ripe describe nouns (busy market, colorful stalls).
- Quickly, eagerly, happily, proudly, carefully, patiently modify verbs, showing HOW actions happen.
- She and they are pronouns replacing longer noun phrases.
- The, every, several, multiple are determiners signaling which nouns we mean.
- Near, between, and at are prepositions showing relationships and locations.
- And, because, and while are conjunctions connecting ideas.

## Summary

Eight major word classes build English sentences: nouns (people, places, things), verbs (actions, states), adjectives (describe nouns), adverbs (modify verbs/adjectives/other adverbs), pronouns (replace nouns), determiners (signal which noun), prepositions (show relationships), and conjunctions (connect ideas). The most important skill is not memorizing labels, but recognizing each word's job in context. The same word can change class depending on how it is used, so always identify function before form.

## Overview

Voice changes perspective, not basic meaning. In active voice, the subject does the action. In passive voice, the subject receives it. Learners need both forms because English uses passive voice naturally in reports, processes, news, and situations where the doer is unknown, obvious, or less important than the result.

## Learning Objectives

- Form passive structures accurately across common tenses.
- Decide when passive voice is genuinely more natural than active voice.
- Move between active and passive without losing tense, meaning, or object relationships.

## Key Rules

- Active voice places the doer in subject position; passive voice places the receiver in subject position.
- Form the passive with the correct form of be + past participle.
- Keep the original tense when switching voices (writes → is written, wrote → was written, has written → has been written).
- Use by + agent only when the doer matters; omit it when the doer is unknown, obvious, or unimportant.
- Not every active sentence should become passive; choose passive when the result, process, or receiver deserves focus.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "The editor approved the article yesterday."
*Active voice focuses on the doer: the editor.*

**Example 2:** "The article was approved yesterday."
*Passive voice focuses on the result; the editor is unimportant or obvious.*

**Example 3:** "The team is designing the new homepage."
*Active present continuous.*

**Example 4:** "The new homepage is being designed right now."
*Passive present continuous keeps the same time meaning.*

**Example 5:** "Someone has stolen my bike."
*Active voice with unknown agent.*

**Example 6:** "My bike has been stolen."
*Passive is more natural when the receiver/result matters.*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "The report was wrote yesterday."
- ✅ RIGHT: "The report was written yesterday."
- Note: Passive needs be + past participle, not simple past.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "The problem solved by the team."
- ✅ RIGHT: "The problem was solved by the team."
- Note: Passive needs an auxiliary verb.

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "English is speaking in many countries."
- ✅ RIGHT: "English is spoken in many countries."
- Note: Passive requires a past participle, not an -ing form.

## Quick Reference

Active: subject + verb + object (The team fixed the bug.)
Passive: subject + be + past participle (+ by + agent) (The bug was fixed.)
Present simple passive: is/are + past participle
Past simple passive: was/were + past participle
Present continuous passive: is/are being + past participle
Present perfect passive: has/have been + past participle
Future passive: will be + past participle
Modal passive: modal + be + past participle (must be signed)
Ask first: who matters more here, the doer or the result?

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: Launch Day at the Studio"

This story shows why writers choose active voice in some sentences and passive voice in others.

"On launch day, the design team tested the app before sunrise. They corrected two navigation bugs, updated the pricing page, and sent the release notes to the support team."

"By noon, the app had been approved by both stores, and the first reviews were being posted online. A small server issue was discovered during the rollout, but it was fixed within twenty minutes."

"In the final team summary, the lead developer wrote actively about decisions and responsibilities, but the public report used more passive voice because the focus was on the product, the release process, and the results."

**Analysis:**

- Active voice highlights who did the work during the launch.
- Passive voice highlights what happened to the app and the process once results became more important than the doer.
- The choice depends on focus, not on whether one form is always better.

## Summary

Use active voice when the doer matters most and passive voice when the receiver, result, or process deserves focus. Passive form requires be + past participle, and the tense must stay aligned with the original sentence.

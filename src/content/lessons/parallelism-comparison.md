## Overview

Parallel structure makes language easier to process and more persuasive. When items are joined in a list, pair, or comparison, they should follow matching forms. Comparisons also require logic: compare like with like, and avoid incomplete or distorted structures.

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize unbalanced forms in coordination and lists.
- Repair faulty comparisons by comparing equivalent categories.
- Use parallel structure to strengthen clarity and rhythm.

## Key Rules

- When two or more items are coordinated, keep them in the same grammatical form.
- Use than and as with structurally complete comparisons when needed for clarity.
- Compare equivalent categories, not mismatched things.
- Parallel structure works with lists, pairs, and comparisons across sentences.
- Breaking parallelism disrupts rhythm and obscures relationships.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "The course teaches students to analyze, to revise, and to edit."
*All three use infinitive form (to + base verb).*

**Example 2:** "Her explanation was clearer than mine."
*Compares two explanations (logical match).*

**Example 3:** "She loves reading, writing, and teaching."
*All three are -ing forms (gerunds).*

**Example 4:** "He is intelligent, hardworking, and generous."
*All three are adjectives.*

**Example 5:** "The team will organize the event, promote it, and evaluate the results."
*All three use the same verb form (future tense).*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "She loves reading, writing, and to teach."
- ✅ RIGHT: "She loves reading, writing, and teaching."
- Note: Don't mix -ing with infinitives; keep all three forms the same.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "He is intelligent, works hard, and generous."
- ✅ RIGHT: "He is intelligent, hardworking, and generous."
- Note: All items should be the same part of speech (three adjectives).

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Her writing is better than John."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Her writing is better than John's. / She writes better than John."
- Note: Compare writing to writing (or person to person), not writing to person.

## Quick Reference

Lists: keep all items in the same form (all nouns, all -ing, all infinitives, all adjectives)
Pairs: use matching forms with and/or/but (talking and walking, not talking and walk)
Comparisons: compare equivalent things (her writing vs. his writing, not her writing vs. him)
Coordinate conjunctions (and, or, but) require parallel structure on both sides
Correlative conjunctions (both...and, either...or, not only...but also) require parallelism
Not only...but also: both sides must use the same grammatical form
Broken parallel: easy to spot because one item sticks out (three -ing forms + one infinitive)
Logical comparison: compare nouns to nouns (apples to apples), not nouns to people

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: The Resume Revision"

This narrative shows how parallel structure creates balance and clarity, and how breaking it causes confusion.

"Maya was revising her resume, and she needed to list her skills: communicating effectively, solving problems creatively, and leading teams. She also wanted to show what she had accomplished: launched three projects, built a strong department, and trained five new managers. The format was important: parallel structure made her achievements clear."

"Her old version had mixed the forms awkwardly: 'Communication skills, problem solving, and the ability to lead.' An interviewer might wonder: Does she communicate well, solve problems, and lead teams? The parallel version—'communicating, solving, leading'—answered directly."

"She also compared her experience to others carefully. Instead of writing 'Her design skills exceed John's writing,' she compared fairly: 'Her design skills exceed his design analysis skills.' Parallel structure wasn't decoration; it was clarity."

**Analysis:**

- Lists with parallel structure are easier to scan and remember (three -ing forms).
- Breaking parallelism creates confusion (mixed infinitive and noun forms).
- Comparisons work only when comparing equivalent things (skill to skill, not skill to person).

## Summary

Parallel structure balances similar ideas using matching grammatical forms. In lists, use the same form; in comparisons, compare equivalent categories.

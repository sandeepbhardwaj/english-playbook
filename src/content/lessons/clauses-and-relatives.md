## Overview

Complex sentences allow you to show cause, contrast, sequence, condition, and additional detail. Relative clauses are especially useful because they let you describe a noun inside the sentence itself.

## Learning Objectives

- Link unequal ideas with subordinators.
- Choose relative pronouns that fit the noun being described.
- Use commas correctly with nonrestrictive clauses.

## Key Rules

- Use subordinating conjunctions such as because, although, if, while, after, and before to link unequal ideas.
- Use who for people, which for things, and that for restrictive clauses.
- Nonrestrictive (extra-info) clauses need commas; restrictive (essential-info) clauses don't.
- Relative pronouns (who, which, that) replace the noun in the relative clause.
- Where and when can replace 'in which' and 'at which' for place and time.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "The tutor who helped me yesterday explained articles clearly."
*Restrictive: which tutor? Answer: the one who helped me.*

**Example 2:** "Although the task was difficult, the class finished on time."
*Although introduces a dependent clause of contrast.*

**Example 3:** "She studied in the library, which was very quiet."
*Nonrestrictive: extra info about the library (commas needed).*

**Example 4:** "The house that we rented was near the beach."
*Restrictive: which house? Answer: the one we rented.*

**Example 5:** "This is the city where my grandparents were born."
*Where replaces 'in which' for locations.*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "The tutor who helped me, explained articles clearly."
- ✅ RIGHT: "The tutor who helped me explained articles clearly."
- Note: Don't use commas around a restrictive clause; it's essential to the meaning.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "The book, which I need to read is on the table."
- ✅ RIGHT: "The book, which I need to read, is on the table."
- Note: Nonrestrictive clauses need commas before AND after.

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Although the weather was bad, but we played football."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Although the weather was bad, we played football."
- Note: Don't use 'but' after 'although' (same idea twice).

## Quick Reference

Subordinators showing reason: because, since, as
Subordinators showing contrast: although, though, even though, while (≠ same time)
Subordinators showing time: after, before, when, while, until, as soon as, as long as
Subordinators showing condition: if, unless, as long as, provided that
Relative pronouns: who (people), which (things), that (can replace who/which in restrictive clauses)
Where and when: can replace 'in which' / 'at which' for places and times
Restrictive (no commas): describes which one (the book that I read)
Nonrestrictive (with commas): adds extra info (My teacher, who is kind, helped me)

## Practice Plan

1. Exercise 1 - Clause Identification: Read a paragraph and underline dependent clauses, labeling each by type (reason, time, contrast, etc.).
2. Exercise 2 - Punctuation: Add or remove commas in ten sentences with relative clauses, justifying each choice.
3. Exercise 3 - Sentence Combining: Join pairs of short sentences into complex sentences using appropriate subordinators.
4. Exercise 4 - Writing: Write five complex sentences, each using a different subordinating conjunction (because, although, while, after, if).
5. Exercise 5 - Dialogue: Complete a conversation that includes at least three dependent clauses and one relative clause.

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: The Study Group"

This narrative uses various clauses to show relationships between ideas and to describe people and places.

"Maya started a study group because she realized that students who worked together learned more effectively. Although some students were hesitant at first, everyone who attended the first meeting came back. The library, which was their usual meeting place, had quiet corners where they could focus."

"They created a system that made their study time productive. After each topic was covered thoroughly, whoever wanted to explain it aloud did so. If someone did not understand, the group would revisit the material until everyone grasped it."

"By exam day, the group had developed such strong confidence that they felt ready. While they studied, they had built genuine friendships that lasted long after the course ended. The success they achieved, which far exceeded their initial expectations, was traced back to the moment they decided to work together."

**Analysis:**

- Because clauses and relative clauses both explain: because tells why, who/which tells what/which person.
- Although introduces contrast; while can show time (during) or similarity.
- Nonrestrictive clauses (with commas) add bonus info; restrictive clauses (no commas) are essential.

## Summary

Dependent clauses connect ideas with more precision than simple coordination. Relative clauses describe a noun from within the sentence. Learn when to use commas: nonrestrictive clauses (extra info) get them; restrictive clauses (essential info) don't.

## Overview

Editing is where grammar knowledge becomes active skill. Instead of producing language slowly from scratch, you scan for tense errors, agreement problems, article mistakes, punctuation faults, and unclear reference.

## Learning Objectives

- Edit by category instead of guessing randomly.
- Explain each correction with a rule or principle.
- Build speed without sacrificing accuracy.

## Key Rules

- Read once for meaning, then scan again for one error type at a time.
- Check verbs first, then noun phrases, then clause structure, then punctuation.
- When you correct an error, state the rule so the learning sticks.
- Scan for repeated patterns in your errors; fix the pattern, not just the instance.
- Reread after editing to confirm the sentence still conveys the original meaning.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "Incorrect: She don't knows the answer. → Corrected: She doesn't know the answer."
*Auxiliary carries the tense and agreement; she + doesn't (3rd person singular).*

**Example 2:** "Incorrect: The students was revising in library. → Corrected: The students were revising in the library."
*Plural subject (students) needs were; the is required before library.*

**Example 3:** "Incorrect: Each of the applicants have submitted their forms. → Corrected: Each of the applicants has submitted their form."
*Each is singular; needs has and form (referring back to each).*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "It was really a good presentation that was gave yesterday."
- ✅ RIGHT: "It was an excellent presentation yesterday."
- Note: Don't just fix grammar; cut redundancy (was...that was; really good → excellent).

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Each students have their own desk and chair."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Each student has their own desk and chair."
- Note: Each is singular; use has (not have). Modern English accepts singular they.

## Quick Reference

Subject-verb agreement: Identify the true subject (singular or plural) before choosing the verb form.
Collective nouns (team, group, data): Can be singular or plural; choose based on meaning
Tense sequence: Conditional if-clauses use present simple; main clause uses will + base
Double negatives: Avoid don't + never, isn't + none, etc. Use only one negation.
Relative pronouns: who/whom/that/which determine the clause structure; ensure agreement
Article use: Reusable nouns need a/an or the; proper nouns and uncountables follow special rules
Passive voice: Check that past participle is correct and auxiliary agrees with subject
Parallel structure: Lists and comparisons must use the same grammatical form

## Practice Plan

1. Exercise 1 - Verb Agreement: Correct ten sentences with subject-verb disagreement.
2. Exercise 2 - Tense Sequences: Fix ten sentences with incorrect tense choices in conditional/sequence scenarios.
3. Exercise 3 - Article & Pronoun Clarity: Correct ten sentences with missing or wrong articles/pronouns.
4. Exercise 4 - Relative Clauses: Fix ten sentences with incorrect relative pronoun or clause structure.
5. Exercise 5 - Timed Rapid Edits: Correct a mixed paragraph with 8–12 errors in 5 minutes.

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: The Draft and the Revision"

This story shows how systematic editing turns confused writing into polished text.

"Mira submitted her first research paper draft. Her teacher returned it covered in red marks. 'There are seventeen grammar issues,' he wrote. 'But they fall into just three patterns: verb agreement errors, tense mistakes, and relative clause problems. If you fix the pattern, you fix most of the paper.'"

"Mira didn't just correct each sentence randomly. Instead, she printed her paper and made three passes. First pass: she searched for every verb and checked if it agreed with the subject. Second pass: she examined every time marker and checked if tenses matched. Third pass: she found every which or who and checked if the relative pronoun and clause structure were right."

"On her revision, Mira reduced errors from seventeen to zero. She also added a note next to each correction explaining the rule. When the teacher reviewed the revision, she said, 'This is excellent. More importantly, you now understand how to edit. That skill will serve you far longer than this one paper.'"

**Analysis:**

- Editing by category (verbs, then tenses, then clauses) is faster and more effective than random proofreading.
- Writing the rule next to your correction cements the learning.
- Systematic editing reveals patterns in errors; fixing the pattern beats fixing one-off mistakes.

## Summary

Error correction is both a proofreading skill and a learning strategy. Scan by category (verbs, tenses, articles, clauses, punctuation), explain the rule behind each fix, and track patterns to prevent future errors.

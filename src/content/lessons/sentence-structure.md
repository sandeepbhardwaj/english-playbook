## Overview

A complete sentence expresses a full thought. Most English sentences need a subject and a finite verb, and many also need an object, complement, or adverbial phrase. Understanding basic sentence patterns helps you avoid fragments, run-ons, and awkward structures. This lesson focuses on the sentence core first, then on the extra parts that complete or expand the idea.

## Learning Objectives

- Distinguish complete clauses from fragments.
- Recognize core sentence patterns used in everyday English.
- Repair run-ons with punctuation and conjunctions.

## Key Rules

- A clause becomes a sentence only when it can stand on its own as a complete thought.
- The most common patterns are subject-verb, subject-verb-object, and subject-linking verb-complement.
- Dependent clauses need a main clause unless they are part of a larger sentence.
- Independent clauses contain both a subject and a finite verb (not -ing or infinitive unless complete).
- Compound sentences join two independent clauses with and, but, or, yet, so (with a comma before the conjunction).
- Complex sentences use a dependent clause plus an independent clause connected by subordinators.
- Some verbs need something after them to complete the meaning: *She put* is incomplete, but *She put the keys on the table* is complete.

## Quick Tests

- Ask: **Who or what is this sentence about?** That usually helps you find the subject.
- Ask: **What is happening, or what state is being described?** That helps you find the finite verb.
- If the group of words begins with **because, although, when, if, while, after**, check whether it still needs a main clause.
- If two complete sentences are pushed together, you probably need a full stop, semicolon, or comma + coordinating conjunction.
- If the sentence feels unfinished after the verb, check whether the verb needs an object, complement, or place expression.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "The meeting ended early."
*Complete SV pattern: subject (meeting), verb (ended).*

**Example 2:** "Because the bus was late, we missed the introduction."
*Dependent clause (Because...) + independent clause (we missed...).*

**Example 3:** "Alicia wrote the report, and her supervisor approved it."
*Two independent clauses joined by and with a comma.*

**Example 4:** "The student who submitted the essay won first place."
*Independent clause with an embedded relative clause.*

**Example 5:** "After the conference ended, the team returned to their offices."
*Dependent adverbial clause (After...) + independent clause (team returned...).*

**Example 6:** "The soup smells wonderful."
*Subject-linking verb-complement pattern: soup (subject), smells (linking verb), wonderful (subject complement).*

**Example 7:** "She put the folder on the desk."
*Subject-verb-object-adverbial pattern: She (subject), put (verb), folder (object), on the desk (required adverbial).*

**Example 8:** "Running through the hall, the children laughed loudly."
*The opening participle phrase adds detail, but the main clause is the children laughed loudly.*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Because the bus was late."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Because the bus was late, we missed the introduction."
- Note: Because... is a fragment. Add an independent clause.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "The meeting ended early, the team dispersed quickly."
- ✅ RIGHT: "The meeting ended early, and the team dispersed quickly."
- Note: Two independent clauses need and, but, or similar (not just a comma).

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "She put on the desk."
- ✅ RIGHT: "She put the book on the desk."
- Note: Some verbs need an object to complete the pattern. **Put** usually needs to say what was put and where.

**Mistake 4:**
- ❌ WRONG: "When the class finished."
- ✅ RIGHT: "When the class finished, the students packed their bags."
- Note: A time clause beginning with **when** is dependent. It needs a main clause.

## Quick Reference

✓ COMPLETE SENTENCE: subject + finite verb (She ran.)
✓ FRAGMENT: lacks subject or finite verb (Because she ran. / Running down the street.)
✓ RUN-ON: two independent clauses with no connector (She ran she fell.)
✓ COMMA SPLICE: two independent clauses joined only by comma (She ran, she fell.)
✓ COMPOUND: IC + and/but/or + IC (She ran, and I cheered.)
✓ COMPLEX: dependent clause + independent clause (Although she ran, I cheered.)
✓ LINKING VERB PATTERN: subject + linking verb + complement (The sky became dark.)
✓ VERB THAT NEEDS MORE: some verbs require an object or place expression (She placed the bag on the chair.)

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: Building a House Project"

Trace how complete and incomplete sentence structures work in instructions and narrative.

"Jamal decided to build a bookshelf in his apartment. He purchased the materials, and he gathered his tools carefully."

"When he read the instructions, he discovered several issues. The manual was unclear, yet he decided to proceed anyway."

"While Jamal measured and cut the wood, his sister arrived with snacks. She watched him work, and she offered helpful suggestions."

**Analysis:**

- Jamal decided is a simple sentence (subject + verb).
- He purchased... and he gathered... is a compound sentence (two independent clauses with and).
- When he read... combines a dependent clause with an independent clause (complex sentence).
- While Jamal measured... is complex (dependent clause of time + independent clause).
- The manual was unclear is a linking-verb pattern: **manual** is the subject, **was** is the linking verb, and **unclear** is the complement.

## Summary

A complete sentence needs a subject and a finite verb, but strong sentence structure also depends on pattern control. Some sentences stop with subject + verb, while others need an object, complement, or adverbial element to feel complete. Fragments lack a complete main clause. Run-ons and comma splices incorrectly join independent clauses. When you check sentence structure, first find the sentence core, then check what else the verb requires.

## Overview

Pronouns should point clearly to the nouns they replace. English questions and negatives also depend heavily on auxiliary verbs such as do, be, and have. When learners miss the auxiliary, sentences sound incomplete or unnatural.

## Learning Objectives

- Avoid unclear pronoun reference in speech and writing.
- Form standard questions with the right auxiliary.
- Build negatives without dropping tense or agreement.

## Key Rules

- A pronoun should agree with its antecedent in number and fit the meaning clearly.
- Most present and past simple questions and negatives need do, does, or did.
- Avoid ambiguous reference when more than one noun could match the pronoun.
- Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, themselves) are used when subject and object are the same.
- Question word order: do/does/did + subject + base verb (Did you finish?).

## Examples

**Example 1:** "Did you finish the worksheet, or didn't you have time?"
*The auxiliary did supports both the question and the negative.*

**Example 2:** "I hurt myself when I fell."
*Myself shows the subject (I) is also the object.*

**Example 3:** "The students enjoy themselves at the party."
*Reflexive pronoun: themselves refers back to students.*

**Example 4:** "Does she understand the instructions?"
*Does (auxiliary) + she (subject) + understand (base verb).*

**Example 5:** "They didn't arrive on time, but we did."
*Did (auxiliary) replaces the full verb and creates an emphatic negative.*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Why you left early?"
- ✅ RIGHT: "Why did you leave early?"
- Note: English questions in statement form need do/does/did.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "I hurt me when I fell."
- ✅ RIGHT: "I hurt myself when I fell."
- Note: Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, etc.) are used only when subject and object are the same.

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Did you finished the homework?"
- ✅ RIGHT: "Did you finish the homework?"
- Note: After did, use the base verb (finish), not the past tense.

## Quick Reference

Subject pronouns: I, you, he, she, it, we, they, who
Object pronouns: me, you, him, her, it, us, them, whom
Reflexive pronouns: myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves
Possessive pronouns: mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs
Question form (simple tense): do/does/did + subject + base verb
Negative form (simple tense): subject + don't/doesn't/didn't + base verb
Emphatic do: 'I do like it' (in contrast), 'She did finish it'
Pronoun agreement: singular pronouns (he, she, it) take singular antecedents; plural (they) take plural

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: The Interview"

This narrative shows how pronouns must remain clear and how questions and negatives depend on auxiliaries.

"Maya arrived for her interview nervously. She had prepared all week, and it showed. Did the interviewer ask difficult questions? Yes, but she answered them confidently. The interviewer herself had come from a similar background, and she recognized what Maya was trying to communicate."

"They discussed the job carefully. Did Maya understand the responsibilities? She repeated them back to confirm. The interviewer didn't doubt that Maya was qualified; he just wanted to be sure she really wanted the role."

"By the end, Maya and the interviewer agreed that they were a good match. Did she get the job? She did, and she was thrilled. The company's decision made sense, and it led to a wonderful first year for everyone involved."

**Analysis:**

- Use auxiliary verbs (did, does, didn't) in questions and negatives; they support the main verb.
- She, he, herself, themselves all refer back clearly to named people.
- Direct answers to yes/no questions use did, does, didn't to echo the question.

## Summary

Pronouns replace nouns and must agree in number. Questions and negatives in simple tenses require do/does/did. Make sure pronouns have a clear, single antecedent to avoid confusion.

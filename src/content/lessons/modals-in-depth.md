## Overview

Modal verbs do more than add grammar. They show attitude. With a small change from must to should, from can to may, or from might to will, the speaker changes force, confidence, politeness, or certainty. This lesson separates the main modal meanings so learners can choose naturally instead of treating all modals as interchangeable.

## Learning Objectives

- Distinguish obligation from advice, permission from possibility, and deduction from certainty.
- Use modal negatives accurately, especially must not vs. do not have to.
- Choose modal forms that match both the meaning and the social tone.

## Key Rules

- Use can and could for ability; can also expresses informal permission and general possibility.
- Use may and might for possibility; may is also common in formal permission contexts.
- Use should and ought to for advice or expectation, not strong obligation.
- Use must and have to for strong necessity; must often sounds more speaker-driven, while have to often sounds more external.
- Use must for strong deduction (She must be tired), not only for obligation.
- Must not means prohibition; do not have to means lack of necessity.
- Most central modals are followed by the base verb without to.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "You should back up your files before updating the system."
*Should gives advice, not a command.*

**Example 2:** "Employees must wear their ID badges inside the lab."
*Must marks a strong rule.*

**Example 3:** "You don't have to stay for the whole meeting."
*No obligation, not prohibition.*

**Example 4:** "She must be exhausted after that flight."
*Must expresses strong deduction.*

**Example 5:** "Can I borrow your charger for ten minutes?"
*Can gives informal permission request.*

**Example 6:** "May I come in?"
*May is more formal permission.*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "You should to rest this weekend."
- ✅ RIGHT: "You should rest this weekend."
- Note: Do not use to after should, must, can, could, may, or might.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "You mustn't come if you don't want to."
- ✅ RIGHT: "You don't have to come if you don't want to."
- Note: Mustn't means 'you are not allowed,' not 'it is optional.'

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "He can be tired after working all night."
- ✅ RIGHT: "He must be tired after working all night."
- Note: For strong deduction, must is the natural choice.

## Quick Reference

Can: ability, informal permission, general possibility
Could: past ability, polite request, softer possibility
May: formal permission, possibility
Might: weaker possibility
Should / ought to: advice, expectation
Must: strong obligation, strong deduction
Have to: necessity, often external rule or situation
Must not: prohibition
Do not have to: no necessity
Pattern: modal + base verb (should go, must leave, can help)

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: Rules at the Research Lab"

This story shows how modals change meaning depending on whether the speaker gives advice, states rules, or makes deductions.

"On her first day at the research lab, Lina received a safety guide. 'You must wear gloves in the chemical room, and you must not enter the cold-storage area alone,' her supervisor explained. 'You should also label every sample clearly, even when you're in a hurry.'"

"Later, Lina asked, 'May I leave my notebook here?' The supervisor nodded. 'Yes, you may. But you can't leave food anywhere near the instruments.' When the lights in one office stayed on long after sunset, Lina whispered, 'Someone must still be working.'"

"By the end of the week, the rules felt natural. Lina no longer confused must not with don't have to, and she noticed how different the tone sounded when she asked, 'Could you check this result for me?' instead of 'Check this result.'"

**Analysis:**

- Must gives strong obligation, while should softens the force into advice.
- May asks for permission; can or could often request action or assistance.
- Must can also show logical deduction, not only obligation.
- The story contrasts prohibition (must not) with simple lack of necessity (do not have to).

## Summary

Modal choice changes meaning and tone. Use can/could for ability and requests, may/might for permission or possibility, should for advice or expectation, must/have to for necessity, must for deduction, and remember that must not is prohibition while do not have to means no obligation.

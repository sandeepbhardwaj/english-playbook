## Overview

Agreement problems often happen when extra words appear between the subject and the verb. To choose the right verb form, find the grammatical head of the subject, then ignore interrupting phrases.

## Learning Objectives

- Find the true head of complex subjects.
- Use singular verbs with formal indefinite pronouns.
- Handle tricky agreement after prepositional phrases and collective nouns.

## Key Rules

- A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb.
- Phrases between subject and verb do not change agreement.
- Everyone, each, and neither are singular in standard formal English.
- Collective nouns (team, group, family) are singular when acting as one unit.
- The number of + countable noun takes a singular verb; a number of takes a plural verb.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "The list of topics is on the table."
*List is the head noun (singular); topics is in a prepositional phrase.*

**Example 2:** "Neither of the answers seems complete."
*Neither is singular in formal English.*

**Example 3:** "The team has won every match this season."
*Team is treated as one unit (singular verb: has).*

**Example 4:** "The students in the class are preparing for exams."
*Students is plural; the prepositional phrase does not change agreement.*

**Example 5:** "A number of people have complained about the noise."
*A number of takes a plural verb (have).*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "The group of friends are going to the beach."
- ✅ RIGHT: "The group of friends is going to the beach."
- Note: Group is singular; prepositional phrases don't change agreement.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Neither of the options work for me."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Neither of the options works for me."
- Note: Neither is singular; needs singular verb.

**Mistake 3:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Everyone are invited to the party."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Everyone is invited to the party."
- Note: Everyone is singular, even though it refers to many people.

## Quick Reference

Singular heads: each, every, everyone, anybody, someone, something
Plural heads: both, many, few, several, others, most (if countable)
Between subject and verb: ignore prepositional phrases (in, on, with, of, etc.)
Collective nouns (team, group, committee): singular verb = unit; plural verb = individuals
Either...or, neither...nor: verb agrees with the NEAREST subject
The number of = singular, A number of = plural
Compound subjects with 'and' = plural, unless they form one idea (bread and butter = singular)
None can be singular or plural depending on what it refers to

## Practice Plan

1. Exercise 1 - Identification: Read a paragraph and underline the true subject and the correct verb form in each sentence.
2. Exercise 2 - Selection: Choose the correct verb form from pairs (is/are, has/have, was/were) based on the head noun.
3. Exercise 3 - Correction: Fix agreement errors in ten sentences, explaining which word is the real subject.
4. Exercise 4 - Writing: Write eight original sentences using different types of tricky subjects (compound, collective, indefinite pronouns, collective nouns).
5. Exercise 5 - Dialogue: Complete a conversation using correct agreement in questions and statements.

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: Planning the School Event"

This story shows how agreement must follow the true subject even when other words complicate the sentence.

"The committee of teachers and parents is planning the annual talent show. Everyone who signed up goes through an audition. A number of acts come from the music program, and the drama group's production is scheduled for the final spot."

"Neither of the main stage microphones works perfectly, so the technical team has to adjust the backup equipment. Each category of acts has its own judging panel. The group of judges agrees to watch at least six acts before voting."

"By Friday, the list of performers has grown to 45 names. Neither the organizers nor the principal was expecting such enthusiasm. A variety of performances, from comedy to dance to poetry, shows how talented the student body is."

**Analysis:**

- Committee, despite having many members internally, is singular and takes is.
- Number of (plural) vs. The number of (singular) create different agreement patterns.
- Neither...nor requires the verb to agree with the nearest subject.

## Summary

Agreement depends on the grammatical head of the subject, not on extra words between subject and verb. Indefinite pronouns (everyone, each, neither) are usually singular. Collective nouns (team, committee) are singular when treated as a unit.

## Overview

Many grammar mistakes are really pattern mistakes. English relies on common combinations such as interested in, depend on, good at, and responsible for. Modifier placement is also important because a poorly placed phrase can produce confusion or unintended humor.

## Learning Objectives

- Learn phrase-level preposition patterns, not isolated words.
- Place modifiers close to the word they describe.
- Notice and repair dangling or misplaced modifiers.

## Key Rules

- Learn prepositions as part of a phrase, not as isolated words.
- Place modifiers close to the word they describe.
- Fronted or final modifiers should still point clearly to the intended noun or verb.
- Dangling modifiers occur when the logical subject of the modifier is missing from the sentence.
- Misplaced modifiers create confusion or unintended humor when they seem to describe the wrong word.

## Examples

**Example 1:** "She is interested in linguistics and good at analysis."
*Adjectives + prepositions: interested in, good at (learn patterns, not isolated prepositions).*

**Example 2:** "Walking through the hallway, I noticed the posters."
*Walking (modifier) has I as the logical subject, so I comes first in the sentence.*

**Example 3:** "We depend on your support, and we believe in your vision."
*Verb-preposition pairs: depend on, believe in.*

**Example 4:** "Hiding behind the door, the cat waited for the mouse."
*Correct: the cat is hiding (logical subject matches the implied subject).*

**Example 5:** "The book on the table with the red cover is mine."
*Two modifiers (on the table, with the red cover) both modify book.*

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:**
- ❌ WRONG: "Walking through the park, the birds seemed beautiful to me."
- ✅ RIGHT: "Walking through the park, I thought the birds were beautiful."
- Note: Dangling modifier: birds aren't walking; I am. Put the doer first.

**Mistake 2:**
- ❌ WRONG: "I saw a man wearing a hat on the bus."
- ✅ RIGHT: "I saw a man wearing a hat on the bus. (Clarify: Who was on the bus? The man or I?)"
- Note: Misplaced modifier: on the bus seems to modify hat, not man or the main action.

## Quick Reference

Adjective-preposition pairs: interested in, good at, afraid of, responsible for, focused on, amazing at, awful at
Verb-preposition pairs: depend on, believe in, listen to, belong to, focus on, insist on, complain about
Participial modifiers: -ing or -ed forms at the start (Feeling confident, she...; Supported by friends, he...)
Relative clauses: who, which, that + verb (The student who studied hard...)
Prepositional phrases: phrase after a noun (The plan with the best results...)
Adverbial modifiers: describe how, when, where (Quickly, she finished. She finished in an hour.)
Appositive modifiers: rename or describe a noun (My friend Sarah, an expert gardener, showed me...)
Test for dangling: Does the sentence subject perform the action in the opening modifier?

## Practice Plan

1. Exercise 1 - Phrase Building: Create ten sentences using adjective-preposition combinations (interested in, responsible for, afraid of, etc.).
2. Exercise 2 - Dangling Modifier Repair: Fix fifteen sentences with dangling modifiers by adding or clarifying the logical subject.
3. Exercise 3 - Modifier Placement: Rewrite ten sentences so modifiers come next to the words they describe.
4. Exercise 4 - Combining Sentences: Join short sentences into longer ones using modifying phrases and clauses.
5. Exercise 5 - Writing: Write five paragraphs describing a location or event, using at least three different types of modifiers.

## Story Lab

### "Story Lab: The Summer Internship"

This story shows how modifiers work in context and why placement matters for clarity.

"Having secured the internship offer in May, Sarah felt relieved. She was interested in marketing and determined to succeed. Her supervisor, impressed by her enthusiasm, assigned her a client project on day one."

"Working on the campaign, Sarah discovered that many details required precision. Her team, responsible for the budget and concerned about timing, met daily. The revision suggested by the client on Friday required reworking an entire presentation."

"Despite the pressure, Sarah persisted and completed the project on time. Proud of her work and satisfied with the feedback, she delivered the final presentation. The manager, delighted with her progress, offered her a full-time role. Approaching the end of summer and excited about what came next, Sarah felt her hard work had paid off."

**Analysis:**

- Participial phrases (Having secured, Impressed by) begin sentences with the sentence subject implied.
- Preposition phrases (on the budget, on day one) modify nouns closely.
- Adjectives + prepositions (interested in, responsible for, concerned about, proud of) create natural, chained descriptions.

## Summary

Modifiers clarify or describe nouns and verbs. Place them close to the word they modify. If a modifier starts a sentence, the subject must be the person or thing doing the action described by the modifier.
